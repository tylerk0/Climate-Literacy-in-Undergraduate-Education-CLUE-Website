Climate Literacy in Undergraduate Education

(100) / 200 / 300 / 400 : Intro-Level

1-2 Weeks / 6 Units

Summary

Exposure to extreme temperatures and humidity is a significant public health concern to all populations, especially in urban areas, where the urban heat island exacerbates extreme heat and increases heat exposure and heat-related illnesses and death. Traditional ambient weather measurement is obtained through hourly weather stations, such as those at airports. However, fixed-location weather stations do not capture the variability of heat within the microclimates of an urban area and, thus, do not identify more vulnerable populations that need increased resources and mitigation before and during heat events.

Students complete an in-depth microclimate curriculum, collect their own heat exposure data, analyze their data and compare their exposure to others in the class, work with fellow students to interpret results, and apply both scientific and sociological concepts to their results. By completing this curriculum, students learn about microclimate conditions actually encountered by college students. Data was collected by students who wore small temperature and humidity pendants suspended from necklaces during a 2-day interval. Instructors are encouraged to reach out to project lead, Dr. Alisa Hass (Alisa.Hass@mtsu.edu), to explore the possibility of borrowing iButton necklaces, so that students can collect their own temperature and humidity data.

Strengths of the Module

  • Students synthesize information derived from many different disciplines to assess controls on temperature and humidity in their surroundings. Accordingly, it works well in introducing non-geoscience students to the methods, data and issues of geoscience, as well as helping geoscience majors appreciate the societal relevance of temperature and humidity and the value of incorporating socioeconomic information and other information in evaluating geoscience problems and solutions.

  • Temperature and humidity data sets are highlighted throughout the module. Students are challenged to consider the application of geoscience data to their personal lives and the lives of others in the communities in which they live.

  • Students are repeatedly challenged to consider complex temperature and humidity problems as part of socio-ecological systems. It is particularly evident in the module when we have students learn what goes into variations in temperature and humidity from one microclimate to another, consider the interplay between temperature, humidity, human and natural materials, and human activities, and understand the processes leading to the generation of urban heat islands.

Students will be able to evaluate temperature and humidity data on scales ranging from their immediate surroundings to the global. In particular, students will be able to:

  • explain how temperature and humidity extremes pose threats to human well-being.

  • explain controls on temperature and humidity and apply these concepts.

  • explain what controls geographic variability in temperate and humidity in microclimates and at regional and global scales.

  • apply geoscience information and methods in interdisciplinary assessments of temperature and humidity as they relate to sustainability.
